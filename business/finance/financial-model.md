## 1. How This Model Works

The business has three revenue layers that build on each other:

1. **Assessments** — small, fast engagements that open the door (Strategy & Advisory, Cloud, Security, Resilience assessments). Low dollar value, high strategic value as a funnel entry point.
2. **Project Delivery** — larger one-time builds (MVPs, cloud modernization, DevSecOps, BCDR programs). The bulk of early-year revenue.
3. **Recurring Revenue (MRR)** — Managed IT, Application Maintenance, BCDR Retainers, and Fractional Executive Advisory. This is the layer that compounds and drives enterprise value (see `sellability-and-valuation.md`).

The strategy is deliberate: **win an assessment, convert it to a project, then convert the project client to recurring managed services.** Each conversion rate is an assumption you should track and improve.

## 9. Cash & Break-Even Narrative

This is a **services business with low capital requirements** — the main cash drivers are timing of receivables and subcontractor payments, not equipment or inventory.

- **Startup capital:** Plan for a conservative **$30k–$60k** buffer to cover tools, insurance (E&O/cyber), legal entity setup, and the first 1–2 months of operating costs before revenue lands. Replace with your real number.
- **Break-even:** In this base case the founder reaches operating break-even **within the first few quarters of Year 1**, once 2–3 projects and the first recurring clients are live. Assessments alone do **not** cover fixed costs — projects and recurring revenue do.
- **Working-capital risk:** The biggest cash trap is **paying subcontractors before clients pay you.** Mitigate with milestone billing, deposits/retainers up front (30–50%), and net-15 to net-30 terms. Recurring clients should be billed **monthly in advance.**
- **The recurring flywheel:** Because recurring revenue is collected in advance and carries lower delivery cost than projects, every new retainer improves cash predictability. By Year 3, ~$155k of MRR collected monthly in advance largely de-risks the cost base.
- **Concentration caution:** Early on, one large project can be >40% of revenue. Treat that as a **risk**, not a win — diversify the client base as fast as quality allows (see KPIs).

> **Bottom line:** The model is cash-generative and capital-light if (a) the founder bills deposits/retainers in advance, (b) subcontractor cost stays near 45% of project revenue, and (c) recurring clients are added steadily and retained. These three levers, not the headline revenue, determine whether the business is healthy.
