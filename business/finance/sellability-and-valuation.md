# B&B Global Services LLC — Sellability & Valuation Guide

**Purpose:** A practical guide to building a company that is *actually sellable*, what acquirers of IT services / MSP firms pay for, the general valuation ranges those firms trade in, and a prioritized 1–3 year checklist to maximize sale value.

The core idea is simple: **you do not get paid a premium for revenue — you get paid a premium for revenue that an acquirer can keep without you.** Everything below is about converting B&B's land-and-expand model into a durable, transferable asset.

---

## 1. What Acquirers of IT Services / MSP Firms Actually Pay For

When a strategic acquirer, a larger MSP, or a private-equity buyer evaluates a services firm, they are pricing *risk-adjusted, transferable future cash flow.* The factors below move the price the most.

| Value driver | Why buyers care | What "good" looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Recurring revenue %** | Predictable, contracted revenue survives the transition and underwrites the purchase. This is the single biggest multiple driver. | **40%+** of revenue recurring; the higher the better |
| **Margins (gross & EBITDA)** | Higher margins = more cash to apply a multiple to and headroom to absorb integration cost. | Gross **50–60%+**, EBITDA **20%+** (sustainable) |
| **Client concentration** | If one client is 30–50% of revenue, losing them post-sale is catastrophic — buyers discount heavily or hold back payment. | **No client > 20%**, top-3 **< 40%** |
| **Contracts / MSAs in place** | Signed, assignable Master Service Agreements with notice periods make the revenue defensible and transferable. | Most recurring revenue under **multi-month/annual MSAs** |
| **Low founder dependence** | If the founder *is* the business — owns every client relationship and does the delivery — there is little to buy. | Delivery and relationships run **without the founder** |
| **Documented processes / playbooks** | Repeatable, documented delivery means quality survives staff turnover and scales. De-risks the asset. | Written **delivery playbooks, runbooks, SOPs** |
| **Clean financials** | Buyers fund diligence on accrual-based, reviewed financials. Messy books kill or discount deals. | **Accrual accounting, clean P&L, separated owner expenses** |
| **Client diversification** | Spread across industries and contract types reduces correlated risk. | Multiple **industries & service lines** |
| **Net revenue retention** | A book that expands on its own (land-and-expand working) is worth more than one that needs constant replacement. | **NRR ≥ 100%**, logo churn **< 10%/yr** |
| **Team & bench depth** | Trained delivery staff/contractors who stay post-close carry the relationships and the work. | Documented roles, a **contractor/staff bench** |

## 2. Illustrative Valuation Multiples (general market context only)

> These are **broad, illustrative ranges** for orientation, **not a quote or guarantee.** They move with interest rates, buyer appetite, firm size, growth, and deal structure. Smaller firms generally sit at the low end; larger, faster-growing, higher-recurring firms at the high end. Confirm current ranges with an M&A advisor.

| Business profile | Common basis | Illustrative range |
|---|---|---|
| **Project-only / staff-aug consulting** (mostly one-time revenue, founder-led) | Multiple of SDE / adjusted EBITDA | **~2x – 4x EBITDA** |
| **Managed services / MSP** (strong recurring, contracted, diversified) | Multiple of adjusted EBITDA | **~4x – 8x EBITDA** (premium MSPs higher) |
| **Product-led / SaaS-heavy** (recurring software, scalable) | Multiple of ARR / revenue | **~3x – 10x+ ARR** depending on growth & margin |

**Reading these:**

- A **pure project shop** is valued mostly on profit and trades at low single-digit EBITDA multiples, because the revenue must be re-won every year and is often founder-dependent.
- An **MSP with high recurring revenue, MSAs, and low concentration** trades at meaningfully higher EBITDA multiples — the *same EBITDA is worth more* purely because it recurs.
- **Adjusted EBITDA (or SDE)** normalizes owner compensation and one-off costs — buyers add back above-market owner pay and non-recurring expenses. Clean books make these add-backs defensible and increase the number the multiple is applied to.

> **The strategic implication for B&B:** moving from a ~17% recurring mix toward **40%+** (the trajectory in `financial-model.md`) can shift the firm from the low-multiple "project shop" category into the higher-multiple "MSP" category — potentially **doubling the multiple on the same EBITDA.** That mix shift is the highest-leverage value-creation move available.

## 3. The Value Equation

Enterprise value is roughly **(Adjusted EBITDA) × (Multiple)** — and the multiple is set by the risk factors in Section 1.

```
Enterprise Value ≈ Adjusted EBITDA × Multiple

Multiple ↑ when:  recurring % ↑ · margins ↑ · concentration ↓ ·
                  founder-dependence ↓ · processes documented ·
                  contracts in place · NRR ≥ 100% · financials clean
```

You can grow value two ways at once: **grow adjusted EBITDA** *and* **raise the multiple** by de-risking the business. The 1–3 year checklist below targets both.

---

## 4. Prioritized 1–3 Year Sellability Checklist

Ordered by leverage. Earlier items unlock the most value per unit of effort.

### Year 1 — Foundations & the recurring shift (highest leverage)

| # | Action | Value driver it improves |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Shift toward recurring.** Convert every project client to a recurring offer (Managed IT, App Maintenance, BCDR retainer, or Fractional Advisory) at handoff. Make recurring the default, not an upsell. | Recurring %, NRR |
| 2 | **Put recurring revenue under signed MSAs** with clear scope, term, renewal, and assignment language. | Contracts in place |
| 3 | **Adopt accrual-based accounting** with a bookkeeper/accountant; separate all personal expenses from the business. | Clean financials |
| 4 | **Start a metrics dashboard** (MRR, gross margin, NRR, concentration) — see `unit-economics-and-kpis.md`. You cannot improve or prove what you do not measure. | All drivers |
| 5 | **Document the top 2–3 delivery playbooks** (assessment, project delivery, onboarding) as written SOPs. | Documented processes |

### Year 2 — Reduce founder dependence & diversify

| # | Action | Value driver it improves |
|---|---|---|
| 6 | **Move delivery off the founder.** Build a trained contractor/staff bench and route delivery through them, not you. Track founder-dependence % and drive it down. | Low founder dependence |
| 7 | **Transfer client relationships** to named account owners so no client is "the founder's account." | Founder dependence, transferability |
| 8 | **Diversify the client base** so no client exceeds ~20% and top-3 stays under ~40% of revenue. | Client concentration |
| 9 | **Expand within accounts** (land-and-expand) to push NRR above 100% — add service lines to existing clients. | NRR, recurring % |
| 10 | **Productize the offer** (named packages, fixed scopes, standard SLAs) so delivery is repeatable and margins are stable. | Margins, processes |

### Year 3 — Polish for diligence & maximize the multiple

| # | Action | Value driver it improves |
|---|---|---|
| 11 | **Get reviewed/audited financials** and maintain a clean, supportable EBITDA add-back schedule. | Clean financials |
| 12 | **Tighten margins** — raise utilization, manage subcontractor cost, reprice underwater work toward target gross/EBITDA. | Margins |
| 13 | **Lock in contract durability** — annual terms, auto-renewals, reasonable notice periods, assignability on change of control. | Contracts in place |
| 14 | **Build a management layer** that can run delivery and sales without the founder for an extended period. | Founder dependence |
| 15 | **Assemble a diligence data room** — financials, contracts/MSAs, org chart, playbooks, KPI history, client list, insurance, IP, and key policies. | Deal readiness |

## 5. The Five Things That Most Often Kill or Discount a Deal

| Deal risk | Why it hurts | Fix (cross-reference) |
|---|---|---|
| **Heavy client concentration** | Buyer fears losing the anchor client post-close → price cut or earn-out holdback | Diversify; cap any client < 20% (Section 4, #8) |
| **Founder is the business** | Nothing transferable to buy → low or no multiple | Off-load delivery & relationships (Section 4, #6–7) |
| **Mostly one-time project revenue** | Revenue must be re-won → low EBITDA multiple | Shift to recurring under MSA (Section 4, #1–2) |
| **Messy financials / commingled expenses** | Buyer cannot trust the numbers → diligence stalls or discounts | Accrual books, clean separation (Section 4, #3, #11) |
| **No documented processes** | Quality walks out the door with any departure | Write playbooks/SOPs (Section 4, #5, #10) |

---

## 6. Bottom Line

B&B's land-and-expand model — **assessment → project → recurring managed services** — is already the right shape for a sellable company *if* the recurring layer is grown, contracted, diversified, and de-coupled from the founder. The work over the next 1–3 years is less about top-line growth and more about **converting revenue into a transferable, low-risk, well-documented asset.** Do that, and the same EBITDA earns a higher multiple — which is where the real exit value is created.

> **If you optimize for one thing:** grow **contracted, diversified recurring revenue that runs without you.** It raises EBITDA *and* the multiple at the same time.
