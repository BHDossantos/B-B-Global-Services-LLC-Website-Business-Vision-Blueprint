## Post 4 — Thought Leadership: DevSecOps for the Mid-Market

**"We'll add security later" is the most expensive sentence in technology.**

For mid-market teams, security often shows up as a late-stage gate — a scan right before launch that surfaces a pile of issues nobody has time to fix.

So one of two things happens: the release slips, or it ships with known risk.

Neither is a good answer.

The fix isn't more meetings or a heavier process. It's moving security *into* delivery instead of bolting it on at the end.

What that looks like in practice:
→ Security checks running inside CI/CD, automatically
→ Secrets managed properly, not pasted into config
→ IAM and access reviewed as part of the build
→ Compliance evidence collected as you go, not scrambled before an audit

Done right, this makes you faster, not slower. Issues get caught when they're cheap to fix — early — instead of expensive and public.

Secure-by-default is achievable for the mid-market. You don't need an enterprise security org to get there.

## Post 5 — Thought Leadership: Business Continuity That Actually Works

**A recovery plan you've never tested is just a guess.**

Most companies have *something* — a backup job, a doc in a shared drive, a vague sense that "we're covered."

Then an outage hits, and the questions get sharp fast:

How current is that backup, really?
How long until we're back online?
How much data can we afford to lose?
Who runs the recovery, step by step?

If you can't answer those calmly, you don't have a continuity plan. You have hope.

Real resilience comes down to a few unglamorous things:
→ Defined RTO and RPO — agreed by the business, not assumed by IT
→ Backups that are validated, not just scheduled
→ Runbooks anyone on the team can follow under pressure
→ Tabletop exercises that prove the plan before a real event does

The goal isn't a binder. It's a tested, defensible capability — and the executive confidence that comes with it.

When did your team last actually *run* a recovery test?

## Post 6 — Thought Leadership: AI Readiness

**Most "AI strategies" right now are just anxiety with a budget.**

There's enormous pressure to "do something with AI." So teams chase demos, buy tools, and end up with pilots that impress in a meeting and disappear a month later.

The mid-market doesn't need more hype. It needs a grounded starting point.

A better first question isn't "what AI tool should we buy?"

It's "where do we lose the most time to manual, repetitive work?"

Start there:
→ Pick one high-volume, rules-heavy workflow
→ Keep a human in the loop for judgment and review
→ Define what "good" looks like before you build
→ Measure real ROI, not vibes

That's how AI goes from a science project to something that actually moves the business — with manageable risk and a clear payback.

You don't need a moonshot. You need one well-chosen problem and a partner who can both scope it and build it.
